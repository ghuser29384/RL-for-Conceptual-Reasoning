Scoring guide

Use the full 0–100 range. The goal is not to agree with the position; it is to assess the critique’s quality and impact on the position as written.

Centrality

How important are the claims attacked by the critique to the position? 100 means the position would collapse if those claims were false.

Strength

How successfully does the critique refute the claims it attacks? Assess the object-level reasoning, not the confidence of its wording.

Correctness

What proportion of the critique’s claims are true? Wrong but irrelevant claims still reduce correctness.

Clarity

After careful reading, how precisely can you determine what the critique is arguing?

Dead weight

How much content contributes neither an argument nor useful clarification? Here, 100 means entirely dead weight.

Single issue

How fully does the critique focus on one issue rather than several independent objections?

Overall

How good is the critique all things considered? Anchor on centrality × strength, then adjust for correctness, clarity, insight, and extraneous material.

Important: Centrality and strength can trade off depending on interpretation. Focus especially on getting their product—how much the critique weakens the position—approximately right.
Blinded calibration review

Read the position and critique closely. The item is used only for calibration and reviewer qualification.

Position

Clearly, one can infer “ought” from “is,” thus bridging the is/ought gap:

  1. “Bertie morally ought to marry Madeline” is one of Aunt Dahlia’s beliefs.
  2. All of Aunt Dahlia’s beliefs are true.
  3. Therefore, Bertie morally ought to marry Madeline.

Both premises are descriptive: the first is about what a person believes, and the second is about the truth-value of that person’s beliefs. The conclusion is moral. The argument is logically valid because necessarily, if its premises are true, its conclusion is true.

Critique

While the argument is logically valid—the conclusion follows from Premises 1 and 2—it does not bridge from “is” to “ought.” After all, Premise 1 already contains an “ought.”

FAQ

Common questions

Do I need a philosophy PhD?

No. Relevant doctoral study, research employment, legal or policy analysis, debate adjudication, advanced teaching, or demonstrated independent research can qualify. Calibration quality matters.

Can I use an AI assistant?

No. The purpose is to obtain independent human judgments. Using an AI assistant, copied analysis, or automated form completion disqualifies the submission.

How is acceptance decided?

Eligibility, completion time, rubric engagement, internal consistency, and the written rationale are reviewed together. Exact numerical agreement with one reference rater is not required.

How will I be paid?

Accepted reviewers receive instructions by email after audit. The form never asks for banking credentials.
